## The Problem

Stick-slip is the dominant source of positioning error and surface-finish degradation in servo-driven industrial machinery operating at low feed rates. When a ball-screw axis traverses the Stribeck velocity zone, the closed-loop system destabilizes into a limit cycle of periodic velocity oscillation, position error, and visible surface patterns.

Existing MPC approaches treat the velocity weight as a tuning parameter. This provides no principled answer to "why this weight?" and no proof of optimality.

## What It Does

FERRUM is an LTV-MPC architecture running at 500 Hz where the velocity weight is derived from thermodynamic first principles — specifically, from the Glue Program's universal crossover constant ξ = ½. The derivation identifies stick-slip dynamics as a specific mathematical structure and proves that a unique weight ratio follows from that structure.

The resulting weight **w_vel = 2 · w_tracking** is not a tuning choice. It is the unique ratio consistent with the thermodynamic properties of the stick-slip process. The paper proves this ratio sits on the optimal Pareto frontier — the point of maximum roughness reduction per unit of position error tolerance.

## Key Design Decisions

**The weight is the claim.** The MPC architecture is standard control engineering. The novel element is that a specific weight ratio is proven to be thermodynamically necessary — not empirically discovered.

**Honest limitations.** Stability guarantee is local. Friction model omits temperature dependence. Hardware validation is pending. The paper states all of this explicitly.

**Industrial deployment path.** 500 Hz matches standard servo drives. The online solve is a small linear system executable on any modern DSP. Directly deployable on CNC machines and precision assembly stages.
