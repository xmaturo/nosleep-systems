## The Problem

Ride comfort in autonomous vehicles is a control problem: the MPC (Model Predictive Control) controller must balance trajectory tracking, safety, and passenger comfort simultaneously. The standard approach is to hand-tune the cost function weights — a tedious process that produces parameters with no theoretical justification. Every time the vehicle dynamics change, the tuning starts over.

The question was whether the cost function weights could be *derived* from a principled mathematical framework rather than manually adjusted.

## Architecture

MOLECULA is an LTV-MPC (Linear Time-Varying Model Predictive Control) architecture where the cost function weights come directly from the Glue Program. The connection is specific:

**Phase-transition dynamics** from the Glue Program's crossover law determine how the controller weights shift between competing objectives (comfort vs. tracking vs. safety) as a function of the driving regime.

**The tanh crossover** maps directly to the weight schedule: in calm conditions (low M/H), the controller prioritizes comfort. As the regime intensifies (high M/H), weights shift toward safety — following the same saturation curve that governs every oscillatory system under detailed balance.

**LTV formulation** allows the controller to adapt in real time. The weights are not static — they evolve with the driving conditions, following a trajectory through the parameter space that the Glue law prescribes.

## Why This Matters

This is a theory-to-product pipeline:

1. **The Glue Program** discovers a universal constant (ξ = ½) governing oscillatory phase dynamics
2. **MOLECULA** turns that constant into a principled weight schedule for an MPC controller
3. **The AV product** uses that controller for ride comfort optimization

The math does not just *inform* the engineering. The math *is* the engineering. The cost function weights are not approximations of the theory — they are direct consequences of it.
