## The Problem

Control Barrier Function (CBF) safety filters are among the most successful tools for certifiable robot safety. A CBF-QP filter solves a quadratic program at each timestep to minimally modify an operator's command while guaranteeing the system stays in a safe set. But there is no universal diagnostic for evaluating whether a safety filter is well-tuned. The question was whether the Glue Program's universal constant ξ = ½ could serve as such a diagnostic.

## The Conjecture — And Its Refutation

The structural resemblance between CBF interventions and PDMP jumps is suggestive: both involve a background flow interrupted by discrete corrections triggered by a state-dependent condition. This motivated the conjecture that CBF intervention statistics obey the Glue law.

**The conjecture fails for deterministic CBF systems.** The hard CBF produces a convex power law |Δθ| ∝ (M/H)² — the qualitative opposite of the concave tanh saturation predicted by the Glue law. The step-function activation boundary breaks the spectral structure that the Glue law requires.

This is where most investigations would stop. The conjecture was wrong. But the failure revealed something deeper.

## The Discovery: A Phase Transition at β* ≈ 3

Replacing the hard threshold with a probabilistic one — P(intervene) = exp(−βd), where β controls the softness — reveals a continuous phase transition in the functional form of the intervention-correction relationship:

| β (softness) | Exponent b | ξ (tanh fit) | R² (tanh) | Regime |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| hard | 1.99 | >20 | 0.87 | Power law (convex) |
| 3.0 | 0.97 | ∞ | 0.96 | **Transition point** |
| 5.0 | 0.51 | 0.53 | 0.96 | Tanh (concave) |

**Below β* ≈ 3:** the system lives in a power law regime. The correction accelerates with M/H. No universal diagnostic exists — the exponent varies depending on which parameter you sweep.

**Above β* ≈ 3:** the system enters a tanh regime. The Glue prediction ξ = 0.527 ± 0.03 emerges with R² = 0.958. The universal constant appears — in a completely different physical context from where it was derived.

**At β* ≈ 3:** the two models tie in explanatory power. The exponent passes through b = 1 (linear). This is the transition point.

## The Prerequisite Paradox

The Glue framework formally requires h(θ) > 0 everywhere — a nonzero hazard rate at every point on the cycle. Adding a background hazard floor h₀ > 0 should improve the result.

It destroys it. At h₀ = 0.01, ξ jumps to 15.8. The Glue law works *better* when its own formal prerequisite is violated. The background interventions far from the obstacle inject noise into bins where corrections are physically meaningless, corrupting the diagnostic.

## By the Numbers

- **β* ≈ 3** — critical softness where the phase transition occurs
- **ξ = 0.527 ± 0.03** at β = 5 with R² = 0.958
- **200,000** timesteps per configuration
- **4** parameter sweeps (CBF gain, safety margin, noise, obstacle geometry)
- **b = 1.99 → 0.51** — power law exponent decreases continuously across the transition

## Key Design Decisions

**Honest methodology.** The conjecture was tested, it failed, and the failure was published alongside the deeper discovery. The paper does not hide the refutation — it leads with it. The negative result is what makes the positive finding credible.

**Design criterion for safety filters.** Filters operating in the tanh regime (β > β*) have a well-defined functional form, a measurable diagnostic (ξ), and a universal target (ξ → 0.5). Filters in the power law regime have a varying exponent that provides no universal benchmark. The phase transition is not just a theoretical finding — it identifies which design choices produce diagnosable safety filters.
